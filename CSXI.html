👋 Welcome Message

Dear students, Welcome to Grade 11 Computer Science! As your educator, I am thrilled to guide you through this fascinating journey into the world of computing. Information and Communication Technology (ICT) is rapidly reshaping our world, and this course is specifically designed to provide you with a robust foundation for future studies and entry into the tech job market.

Computer Science is inherently hands-on. To succeed this year, you must understand both the theoretical principles of technology and how to apply them practically. Let’s look at how our course is structured and what is required of you to successfully complete it.

📊 Course Grading & Assessment Structure

Your final evaluation is split equally between your performance in the classroom and your hands-on laboratory capabilities:

50
Theory (Written Examination) Marks
50
Practical Marks (Internal & External)
⚠️ Important Breakdown: The 50 practical marks are split into two components. 25 marks come from your ongoing Internal Evaluation (classroom participation, terminal exams, and your project report/presentation). The remaining 25 marks come from an External Practical Examination where you will be tested on your writing process, live demonstration of tasks, and a viva voce.

📚 Syllabus Outline & Practical Involvement

Our curriculum is divided into 8 core content areas. While some units focus primarily on fundamental concepts, others involve significant laboratory practice. Below is the mapping of units with their involvement status:

Unit 1: Computer System

Evolution and classification of computers, hardware components (CPU, ALU, CU, Registers), Memory types (Primary, Secondary, Cache), and Computer Architecture (Von Neumann model).

Theory Focus

Unit 2: Number System & Boolean Logic

Binary, Octal, Decimal, and Hexadecimal conversions. 1's and 2's complement arithmetic. Introduction to Boolean logic, logic gates, and truth tables.

Theory & Numericals

Unit 3: Computer Software & Operating System

System and Application software. OS functions and types. Navigation of GUI-based OS and introduction to CLI with basic Windows CMD practice.

Theory & CMD Practice

Unit 4: Application Package

In-depth features of Word Processing (Mail Merge, Tables), Spreadsheets (Complex Formulas, Functions, Charts), and Presentation tools.

Highly Practical

Unit 5: Programming Concepts & Logics

Program design tools (Algorithms, Flowcharts, Pseudocode). C Programming: Data types, Variables, Control structures (If-else, Switch, Loops).

Highly Practical (C)

Unit 6: Web Technology I

Foundations of WWW and HTTP. Building static web pages using HTML5 tags and styling with basic CSS properties.

Highly Practical (HTML/CSS)

Unit 7: Multimedia

Exploration of multimedia elements: Text, Graphics, Audio, Video, and Animation. Discussion on applications in education and industry.

Theory & Discussion

Unit 8: Information Security & Cyber Law

Security threats (Viruses, Phishing) and countermeasures. Overview of digital ethics, Cyber Law, and the Electronic Transaction Act of Nepal.

Theory Focus

📝 Critical Course Requirement: The Lab Report

To successfully complete this course and be eligible for your practical marks, you are strictly required to submit a comprehensive lab report at the end of the academic year.

This report is a compiled, systematic documentation of all the laboratory experiments, coding challenges, website mockups, and software exercises you perform throughout the term.

What your Lab Report must include:

  • The Writing Process: The step-by-step methodology, algorithms, or setup procedures used for each practical task.
  • The Deliverables: Clean source code (for C programming and HTML/CSS), data sheets, or edited media logs.
  • The Outcome: Proof of working execution (such as program outputs or screenshots of built layouts).

Please do not leave this until the last minute. Treat every lab session as a step toward building your final report.
